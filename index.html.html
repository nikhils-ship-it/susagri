01 Concepts & framings

What falls under sustainable & regenerative agriculture?

Sustainable agriculture is not a single technique but a family of practices that strengthen ecological processes, build farmer knowledge, and invest in long-term farm structure. In field surveys across India, three pathways consistently emerge — each addressing a different constraint within smallholder farming systems.

E

Ecological practices

Work by strengthening natural processes — soil regeneration, moisture retention, biological activity, biodiversity. Impact is cumulative, building resilience over time rather than delivering immediate isolated gains.

ExamplesAgroforestry · Cover cropping & mulching · Crop diversity · Vermicomposting
K

Knowledge-led practices

Depend primarily on the farmer's capability — observation, judgment, adaptation. Effectiveness is shaped by how well farmers apply techniques in response to changing conditions, making continuous learning critical.

ExamplesBio-input adoption · Integrated pest management · Seed saving · Intercropping design
S

Structural practices

Involve physical or infrastructural changes to the farm system, typically requiring higher upfront investment. They deliver significant efficiency gains, but adoption depends on access to capital and technical support.

ExamplesRainwater harvesting · Micro-irrigation · Orchard rejuvenation · Integrated farm systems
Definition · Sustainable Agriculture
A system, not a single act.
A production approach that meets present food and livelihood needs without depleting the soil, water, biodiversity, or social fabric that future production depends on. It prioritises low external inputs, on-farm nutrient cycling, contextual adaptation, and farmer agency — moving away from a one-input-fits-all model.
Definition · Regenerative Agriculture
Goes one step further.
Beyond sustaining, regenerative practice actively rebuilds the ecological base — soil organic matter, water tables, biodiversity, microbial life. It treats the farm as a living system and measures success by whether it leaves the land healthier than it was found. Most ecological practices documented here are regenerative in this sense.
01b The nomenclature, mapped

What actually falls under each name?

"Sustainable" and "regenerative" overlap, but they are not identical. Sustainable agriculture aims to not deplete; regenerative agriculture aims to restore. Every regenerative practice is sustainable — but not every sustainable practice is actively regenerative. Here is how the 15 documented practices map across the two overlapping circles.

Sustainable

  • Goal: Maintain productivity without depleting
  • Soil: Prevent further degradation
  • Inputs: Reduce reliance on synthetics
  • Water: Use efficiently
  • Biodiversity: Protect what exists
  • Measure: Net-zero ecological harm

Overlap

  • Goal: Long-term system health
  • Soil: Stabilise structure & microbiology
  • Inputs: Replace synthetic with bio-based
  • Water: Capture, store, distribute well
  • Biodiversity: Maintain & enhance
  • Measure: Resilience to shocks

Regenerative

  • Goal: Actively rebuild the ecological base
  • Soil: Increase soil organic carbon, microbial diversity
  • Inputs: Eliminate external dependencies
  • Water: Restore hydrology, recharge aquifers
  • Biodiversity: Multiply species in farm system
  • Measure: Net-positive — leave land healthier

Read this carefully: Most of the 15 practices documented here qualify as both. The split is conceptual, not exclusive — practices like vermicomposting and agroforestry are sometimes called "sustainable" in one report and "regenerative" in another. 02 Problems & solutions

Eight problems Indian smallholders face. How sustainable practice answers each.

Across nine states and dozens of FGDs, the same problem statements surfaced again and again. The right side of each card maps the practices farmers are actually using to address them — every linked practice opens its full profile, with steps and technology.

Soil degradation & biological death

"The soil cannot recover its own fertility because chemicals prevent the biological processes that would enable recovery."

Decades of synthetic fertiliser have hardened topsoil, killed earthworms, broken nutrient cycling, and reduced water-holding capacity. Farmers report soils that are "hard, rocky, and unable to retain water" — a vicious cycle where declining biology demands ever more chemical input.

Rising input costs & debt cycles

"Expenses have increased while income has decreased over 20 years. Farming has shifted from a profit activity to one where avoiding loss is the realistic goal."

Chemical fertilisers, pesticides, and hybrid seed bills consume ₹12,000–25,000 per acre per season. Retailer-pushed over-application drives many smallholders into perpetual credit cycles — borrowing each season to fund inputs that further degrade their land.

Source · ISSAI Vadamadurai · WoTR Beldal · Cost Analysis Brief

Water scarcity & groundwater decline

"Borewell flow has fallen from four inches to one inch. Ponds that reliably filled have not filled for five consecutive years."

Water-intensive cropping (sugarcane, paddy, banana) combined with unmetered electricity has driven groundwater tables down across Karnataka and Tamil Nadu. April-to-August is described as "very, very difficult" — borewells run dry, even cattle lack drinking water.

Source · MSSRF Kannivaadi · SCOPE Dharwad · MYRADA Kalaburagi
Solutions in praxis
Rainwater Harvesting~2 lakh L from a 1-acre catchment in 800mm rainfall Micro-IrrigationDrip cuts water use 30–60% vs. flood irrigation MulchingReduces evapotranspiration 35–45% System of Rice / Millet IntensificationAlternate wetting & drying — major water savings

Erratic rainfall & climate volatility

"Rain that used to come in January now comes in February or March. Nothing can be done accordingly."

Monsoon onset is shifting; intra-seasonal dry spells and unseasonal harvest-time rain destroy crops. Gaja cyclone (2018) introduced two new permanent pests to Tamil Nadu. Heatwaves push temperatures past 42°C in dryland districts.

Source · Vadakkadu FGD · MSSRF Dindigul · SFM Report
Solutions in praxis
Crop Diversity & RotationMultiple crops hedge against any single failure AgroforestryTree canopy buffers heat and wind extremes Cover Crops & MulchingSoil moisture buffer through dry spells Rainwater HarvestingStored buffer for delayed/erratic monsoon

Worsening pest & disease pressure

"Brown plant hopper and leaf folder outbreaks in rice are linked to unpredictable weather patterns. Pesticide-resistant pests are becoming common."

Synthetic pesticides have produced resistant pest populations, killed natural predators, and accelerated outbreaks of new vectors. Climate stress amplifies disease pressure — humidity-linked diseases like onion downy mildew now appear in seasons that were once safe.

Source · ISSAI Vadamadurai · SFM Report · MSSRF Pudukkottai
Solutions in praxis
Integrated Pest ManagementPheromone traps + neemastra + biocontrol agents Bio-Input AdoptionDashparni + Agni Astra: ₹50/tank vs. ₹300 chemical Crop Diversity & RotationBreaks pest reproductive cycles across families AgroforestryDiverse canopy harbours spiders & parasitoids

Market exploitation & price volatility

"We are getting all the energy for production. But we are not getting the prices for the products we produce."

Without storage, smallholders sell at harvest when prices are lowest. Middlemen exploit distress sales. Onion and maize prices collapse below cost of production. The 40–60% of potential revenue lost at the farm gate is the single biggest economic leak in Indian smallholder farming.

Source · SFM Report · ROSE FPO Pudukkottai · Direct-to-Market ISAP
Solutions in praxis
Post-Harvest Value Addition2–4× price gain through cleaning, grading, packaging Integrated Farming SystemMultiple income streams reduce single-crop dependency Mushroom CultivationYear-round income independent of crop seasonality Crop Diversity & RotationStaggered harvest across 4–6 windows hedges price

Loss of seed sovereignty & traditional varieties

"Hybrid seeds need new inputs every year. The seed our grandmothers kept survived two-three timely rains where new crops fail entirely."

Hybrid seed dependence locks farmers into annual purchase cycles and into varieties bred for ideal — not local — conditions. Traditional landraces that survived erratic rainfall and pest cycles are disappearing from Indian farms within a single generation.

Source · GREEN Foundation · Kudumbam · AHIMSA · CREATE SRI
Solutions in praxis
Seed SavingOn-farm selection, treatment, storage of local varieties Crop Diversity & RotationReintroduces millet, pulse, oilseed landraces Nutrition GardenHousehold seed bank for traditional vegetables System of Rice / Millet IntensificationBrings traditional drought-tolerant millets back
08
⚘

Farmer health & food contamination

"Food was once medicine. Chemical farming has turned it into poison — for our children's bodies, and for the water under our land."

Pesticide exposure has measurable health impacts on farming families — respiratory illness, infertility, infant disease. Chemicals leach into groundwater and surface water. Salinity development on chemical farms makes water "taste different" and contaminates drinking sources.

Source · Devegowda · Pandavapura (Mandya) · INM Kannivaadi FGD
Solutions in praxis
Bio-Input AdoptionZero synthetic chemicals enter farm or food chain Integrated Pest ManagementBotanical sprays — safe for handlers and consumers Nutrition GardenChemical-free vegetables for family consumption Integrated Farming SystemClosed-loop system — no external chemical entry
The pattern

No single practice solves any single problem cleanly. What works is integration. The same farm that adopts bio-inputs (addressing soil + cost + pests + health all at once) also tends to add a nutrition garden, seed saving, and a small rainwater structure. The economic logic and the ecological logic align — every practice that reduces input cost also rebuilds soil; every practice that captures water also reduces pest pressure indirectly. This is why the source documentation consistently describes sustainable agriculture as a system, never a checklist.
